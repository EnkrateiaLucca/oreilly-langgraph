🚀 Alien Anatomy

An octopus’s anatomy reads like science fiction: it has three hearts, nine brains, and blue blood. Two of its hearts pump blood to the gills, while the third sends blood to the rest of the body. Its nervous system is split between a central brain and large nerve centers in its arms, with two-thirds of its neurons located in those arms. The result is a creature whose body is built for extraordinary coordination, making reality seem stranger than fiction.

🔬 Mind of a Cephalopod

Octopuses seem to think in ways that are both fascinating and unfamiliar, and researchers have tested their intelligence by examining how they behave and solve problems. These animals are often described as having nine brains and three hearts, but their real standout traits are what they can do: solve puzzles, use tools, and vanish in plain sight. Studies also show that octopuses can recognize individual humans, which suggests a surprising level of memory and awareness. Taken together, these findings reveal a cephalopod mind that is highly adaptable, observant, and capable of advanced behavior.

💡 Outsider Evolution

Outsider evolution in cephalopods looks less like a side branch and more like a genomic remix. In a study comparing the genomes of two squids, *Doryteuthis pealeii* and *Euprymna scolopes*, and the octopus *Octopus bimaculoides*, researchers produced chromosomal-level assemblies that let them examine cephalopod gene content in detail. Those comparisons suggest that dramatic genome rearrangements created new gene orders that were important in squid and octopus evolution. The work also opens a path to asking how large-scale genome changes may have helped produce traits cephalopods share with vertebrates, including large bodies and disproportionately large brains.

📊 Weird Biology

Octopuses have one of the strangest biological tricks in the animal kingdom: they can rewrite their genetic messages after they’ve been written. Instead of leaving RNA untouched, they extensively edit it, altering the instructions that cells use to make proteins. In their nervous systems, this editing is especially dramatic, with octopuses changing up to 60% of neural RNA sequences. That means they can generate thousands of unique protein variants, a striking example of adaptive molecular-level intelligence.

Peer-reviewed research has made the octopus even stranger: its brain evolved completely independently from the complex mammalian brain, giving scientists a rare window into how sophisticated cognition can arise in a soft-bodied cephalopod. In the octopus genome study, researchers found an unusually large genome with about 33,000 protein-coding genes—more than the fewer than 25,000 in humans—which helped explain why this animal seems almost alien in its biology. Other studies highlight that octopuses are adept at RNA editing and have 42 novel miRNA families in neural tissue, mostly in the brain, suggesting unusual genetic and regulatory mechanisms tied to development and intelligence. Together with traits like decentralized nervous systems, camouflage, and bizarre anatomy, the octopus remains one of the best examples of how evolution can produce a creature that feels like a challenge to everything we know about life.
